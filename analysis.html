What to Expect from March at Selkirk

36 seasons of mountain weather data (1989-2026). Scores reflect best available terrain each day, because that's what guides always pick.

The bottom line: February is the safer bet. It wins 75% of seasons all-time and 90% in the last decade. March meets a "good trip" threshold only 60% of the last 10 years, with 4 failures in 10 seasons. The gap is real, and recent trends are making it worse.

The Question

Selkirk Snowcat Skiing runs 21 tours per season from December through April. I switched from Tour #2512 (February 20-26) to Tour #2516 (March 16-22). After a terrible March trip in 2025/26, I wanted to know: is March really worse, or did I just get unlucky?

How the Model Works

The model scores each day using a gated multiplicative approach. Components aren't interchangeable: you can't make up for zero snow with great safety.

  • Snow quality = best of fresh snow (48h) or preserved older snow (discounted 15%). You need one or the other for good skiing.
  • Safety acts as a terrain access multiplier. Great powder on HIGH danger terrain gets crushed (0.4-0.7x). Low danger = full score.
  • Base depth is a gate. Below 50% adequacy, score drops linearly. Above that, it doesn't matter.
  • Crystal quality is a minor adjustment (±10%).

Scores reflect best available terrain: guides pick the optimal zone each day (Peak north face, A-Frame north trees, A-Frame average, or Lodge trees). The floor is set at 75, calibrated to my worst good February trip (85.8), with a margin for "I'd still be happy with decent snow, good stability, and good preservation."

March is bimodal. The last 10 March seasons split cleanly: six scored 82-91 (great), four scored 15-73 (fail). There's nothing in between. When March works, it's excellent. When it doesn't, it fails hard.

Why March Fails (When It Does)

February and March get similar snowfall over the full record. The difference is what happens after it snows. February averages -8.8C at A-Frame. March averages -4.3C, and in recent years -3.0C.

February: Snow falls at -10C. Five days later, still powder. Crystals stay intact, surface stays dry. Even February's warmest days rarely push A-Frame above freezing.

March: Snow falls at -5C. Most days that's fine. But 2-3 days per tour push A-Frame above 0C. One warm day creates a melt-freeze crust. One rain event can destroy the snowpack in hours.

With the multiplicative model, the worst Februaries are actually too much snow seasons: 2011/12 had 78cm but safety of 34 (HIGH danger), so the terrain multiplier crushed the score to 57.8. Guides couldn't access the steep goods. March failures are different: warm events that destroy the snowpack entirely, leaving no terrain escape.

What Climate Might Be Doing

What Published Research Shows

The station record is too short to detect climate trends. But decades of published research fills the gap:

The established science: BC's interior mountains have warmed ~1.5-1.9C since 1948, roughly 0.25C per decade (Canada's Changing Climate Report, 2019). Spring warming is ~1.8C over 1948-2007. Snowpack (SWE) is declining 5-7% per decade in the Southern Interior, significant at 95% confidence (BC Environmental Reporting). Najafi et al. (2017) formally attributed BC's spring SWE decline to anthropogenic forcing.

Needham et al. (2015) studied Rogers Pass and Mt. Fidelity specifically, finding decreasing 24-hour new snow amounts and increasing early-season rain crusts. A 1.8C spring warming translates to roughly 270m of rain/snow line rise, which is the difference between snow at A-Frame and rain at A-Frame during a marginal storm.

Putting It Together

The last 10 years aren't a clean monotonic decline. What's most likely happening is three things compounding:

  1. Real background warming of ~0.25C/decade has cumulatively raised spring temperatures by ~1C since 1990, pushing March closer to the 0C threshold.
  2. Natural decadal variability masked the trend for two decades. The 2000s were a cold anomaly that made March look better than its long-term trajectory.
  3. The current regime combines accumulated warming with typical variability, resulting in March temperatures that more frequently cross 0C at A-Frame.
It's not purely climate change OR statistical noise. It's both. The warming trend is real (published, attributed, significant across large datasets), but the specific decade-to-decade pattern at this location is dominated by natural variability. The 2000s were likely the anomaly, not the 2010s-2020s.

What to Expect

Good March (~60% of recent seasons): A cold pattern holds. Temps stay below -5C at A-Frame. Storms cycle through normally. Guides route to north faces where snow quality is excellent. Scores land in the 82-91 range. The best March trips rival the best of any month.

Bad March (~40% of recent seasons): A warm event pushes the rain/snow line above A-Frame. Snowpack degrades fast. HIGH avalanche danger closes steep terrain. Scores drop below 73 with no middle ground. 2018/19 (36.1), 2023/24 (15.4), and 2025/26 (25.3) are this pattern.

Signs of a Bad March

Watch the freezing level forecast the week before your trip. When it climbs above 2,000m during a precipitation event, that's rain at A-Frame. The damage is immediate (hours, not days) and recovery requires sustained cold plus new snow.

The Data Behind This

  • London Ridge Low (2,070m, PCIC station): Primary A-Frame data. Actual temp, snowfall (cumulative gauge, positive-difference method), and wind with only 170m elevation adjustment. 35 years.
  • Lardeau High (2,518m, PCIC station, 0.6km from summit): Primary Peak data. Temp and wind, 42m elevation adjustment. 2020-2025.
  • Open-Meteo ERA5 reanalysis (2,529m grid): Gap-fills where stations have no data. Snowfall recalibrated against London Ridge Low ground truth (non-linear correction).
  • Revelstoke Mountain Resort (2,225m, 13 seasons): 20% weight in snowfall blend.
  • Avalanche Canada (1,293 days, 10 seasons): Trains the ML safety model.
  • Cross-validated against 7 PCIC stations at r > 0.92 for temperature. Far stations (44-49km) add no information over the close pair.
