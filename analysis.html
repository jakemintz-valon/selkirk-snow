When to Book Selkirk Snowcat Skiing

A data-driven analysis comparing February and March tours across 36 seasons of mountain weather data.

The bottom line: February is the insurance policy. March is the gamble. With good guides picking terrain, March delivers a great experience 86% of the time. But the 14% failure rate -- when a warm event hits -- has no escape route. February almost never fails.

The Question

Selkirk Snowcat Skiing runs 21 tours per season from December through April. I used to go during Tour #2512 (February 20-26) and recently switched to Tour #2516 (March 16-22). After a terrible March trip in 2025/26, I wanted to understand: how much riskier is March, and what does the data actually say?

The Data

I built a model using 36 seasons (1989-2026) of weather data from multiple sources:

  • Open-Meteo ERA5 reanalysis at 2,529m grid elevation (temperature, snowfall, wind)
  • Lardeau High weather station at 2,518m, less than 1km from the summit (actual temperature and wind observations, 2015-2025)
  • Revelstoke Mountain Resort daily snowfall at 2,225m (13 seasons, calibrates the reanalysis snowfall which underestimates by 2.5x)
  • Avalanche Canada real danger ratings at 3 elevation bands (10 seasons)
  • Environment Canada valley stations for lower elevation conditions

The model scores each day on five components: fresh snowfall (30%), snow preservation (30%), crystal quality (10%), base depth (10%), and avalanche safety (20%). The safety component is trained on 1,293 days of real avalanche danger ratings using machine learning.

Why March Fails (When It Does)

The conventional wisdom is that February gets more snow. That's barely true. At Selkirk's elevation, February and March receive similar snowfall (~2cm/day ERA5, ~5-6cm/day corrected). The Selkirk Mountains are in BC's interior wet belt -- storms keep coming through March.

The real difference is what happens after it snows. February averages -9.5C at the peak. March averages -5.7C. Both cold enough for snow to fall as snow. The difference is preservation:

February: Snow falls at -10C. Next day it's still -10C. Five days later, still powder. The crystals stay intact, the surface stays dry.

March: Snow falls at -6C. Most days that's fine. But 2-3 days per tour, the sun pushes A-Frame above 0C. One warm day creates a melt-freeze crust. One rain event can destroy the entire snowpack in hours.

March's failure mode is binary. In 60% of years, a cold pattern holds and March performs like February. In 40%, a warm event hits. Your 2025/26 tour was a textbook failure: 44mm of rain at the valley on March 17, rain reaching A-Frame, snow depth crashing from 140cm to near zero, HIGH avalanche danger at all elevations for 5 straight days.

The Terrain Selection Factor

At Selkirk, guides choose where to ski each day. In March, they route to north-facing slopes at high elevation where snow persists longer. This terrain selection dramatically changes the equation.

The terrain premium in March (+11.7 points) is 2.5x larger than in February (+8.8) because aspect matters more when the sun is stronger. On a north face at Meadow Peak (2,560m), March conditions rival February.

But terrain can't overcome avalanche danger. When HIGH danger shuts down steep terrain, you're restricted to mellow runs. The model applies a 35% penalty for avi-restricted terrain -- good snow on terrain you can't ski doesn't count.

Your Floor for a Good Experience

I've been to Selkirk 8 times in 9 years. 6 February trips and 2 March trips. All but the 2025/26 March were good. My worst February (2023/24, score 58.1) was still a solid trip. That 58 is my floor for what I'd consider "worth it."

The Decision Framework

Book February if: You're risk-averse, booking a year in advance, and want confidence that the trip will be good regardless of weather. 94% floor rate, no catastrophic downside.

Book March if: You're okay with a ~14% chance of a warm event ruining the week. When March works (86% with good guides), you get nearly identical skiing to February plus longer days and often bigger late-season storms. The best March trips rival the best February trips.

What I Can't Predict

This model uses historical weather patterns, not seasonal forecasts. It can't tell you whether YOUR specific March trip next year will be good or bad. What it can tell you is the base rate: over 36 seasons, March delivers about 6 in 7 times with good guides. February delivers about 15 in 16. Both are good odds. It's a question of how much downside risk you're willing to accept for a $10,000+ trip.
