What to Expect from March at Selkirk

36 seasons of mountain weather data (1989-2026). Scores reflect best available terrain, because that's what guides always pick.

The bottom line: Across 36 seasons, February and March are nearly tied when guides pick optimal terrain. But in the last 10 years, March has gotten meaningfully worse. February wins 80% of recent seasons, and March fails to meet a "good trip" threshold 30% of the time. The climate trend matters more than the all-time average.

The Question

Selkirk Snowcat Skiing runs 21 tours per season from December through April. I switched from Tour #2512 (February 20-26) to Tour #2516 (March 16-22). After a bad March trip in 2025/26, I wanted to know: is March really worse, or did I just get unlucky?

How the Model Works

Scores reflect the best terrain available each day. Guides don't ski a fixed elevation. If it's only good up high, they go high. If trees are better, they stay in the trees. The model picks the best of four zones (Peak north, A-Frame north trees, A-Frame average, Lodge trees) each day, with penalties when avalanche danger restricts steep terrain.

Components: fresh snowfall (30%), snow preservation (30%), crystal quality (10%), base depth (10%), avalanche safety (20%). Safety is trained on 1,293 days of real Avalanche Canada danger ratings.

February has been rock-solid: median 80.1, never below 60.5, tight IQR. The key numbers: Over 36 seasons, March meets the floor 92% of the time. In the last 10 years, that drops to 70%. February has met the floor 100% in both windows.

Why March Fails (When It Does)

February and March receive similar snowfall over the full record. The difference is preservation. February averages -8.8C at A-Frame. March averages -4.3C, and in recent years -3.0C. Both are cold enough for snow to fall as snow. The question is whether it survives.

February: Snow falls at -10C. Five days later, still powder. Crystals stay intact, surface stays dry. Even February's warmest days rarely push A-Frame above freezing.

March: Snow falls at -5C. Most days that's fine. But 2-3 days per tour push A-Frame above 0C. One warm day creates a melt-freeze crust. One rain event can destroy the snowpack in hours. Even the best terrain can't escape this when the freezing level climbs above 2,000m.

March's failure mode is binary. When a cold pattern holds, March performs like February. When a warm event hits, there's no terrain escape. The guides can route to north-facing slopes at high elevation, but if rain reaches 2,000m+, the entire mountain is compromised.

What Climate Might Be Doing

The 2000s and 2010s were a cold, snowy anomaly: fewer days above 0C, 35% more snowfall, and failure rates as low as 22%. The 2020s look bad, but the 1990s also had high failure rates (55%). The 5-year rolling average is at its all-time low (50.4), but the 1990s hit 47.2. On station data alone, you could argue this is just natural variability returning to baseline after an unusually good two decades.

One signal is harder to dismiss: the warm tail is growing. Days above 3C at A-Frame in March went from 0% during 1990-2005 to 1.5% during 2010-2025. March melt events are accelerating at 1.3% per decade, the fastest rate of any month. These are the days that destroy snowpacks.

What Published Research Shows

The station record is too short and noisy to detect climate trends. But decades of published research on BC's interior mountains fills the gap:

The established science: BC's interior mountains have warmed ~1.5-1.9C since 1948, roughly 0.25C per decade. Spring warming is ~1.8C over 1948-2007, less than winter's 3.0C but still substantial. Snowpack (SWE) is declining 5-7% per decade in the Southern Interior mountains, significant at 95% confidence. Najafi et al. (2017) formally attributed BC's spring SWE decline to anthropogenic forcing.

Needham et al. (2015) studied Rogers Pass and Mt. Fidelity specifically, finding decreasing 24-hour new snow amounts and increasing early-season rain crusts. A 1.8C spring warming translates to roughly 270m of rain/snow line rise, which is the difference between snow at A-Frame and rain at A-Frame during a marginal storm.

The station record can't detect a 0.25C/decade signal because 36 years of mountain weather data is dominated by year-to-year and decadal variability. You'd need 60+ years of clean data to pull that signal out of the noise. But the signal is real: it's been measured across hundreds of stations and confirmed via formal attribution studies.

Putting It Together

The last 10 years aren't a clean monotonic decline. The 1990s also had high failure rates. What's most likely happening is three things compounding:

  1. Real background warming of ~0.25C/decade has cumulatively raised spring temperatures by ~1C since 1990, pushing March closer to the 0C threshold at A-Frame.
  2. Natural decadal variability masked the trend for two decades. The 2000s-2010s were a cold/snowy anomaly that made March look better than its long-term trajectory.
  3. The current 2020s regime combines the accumulated warming with a return to more typical variability, resulting in March temperatures that more frequently cross 0C at A-Frame.
It's not purely climate change OR statistical noise. It's both. The warming trend is real (published, attributed, significant across large datasets), but the specific decade-to-decade pattern at this station is dominated by natural variability. The practical implication: the 2000s-2010s were likely the anomaly, not the 2020s. March conditions are unlikely to return to that golden era.

What to Expect

Good March (~70% of recent seasons): A cold pattern holds. Temps stay below -5C at A-Frame. Storms cycle through normally. Guides route to north faces and high elevation where conditions are excellent. Longer days mean more skiing. The best March trips rival the best of any month.

Bad March (~30% of recent seasons): A warm event pushes the rain/snow line above A-Frame. Snowpack degrades fast. HIGH avalanche danger closes steep terrain at all elevations. There's no terrain escape. 2023/24 (score 26.6) and 2025/26 are this scenario.

Signs of a Bad March

Watch the freezing level forecast the week before your trip. When it climbs above 2,000m during a precipitation event, that's rain at A-Frame. The damage is immediate (hours, not days) and recovery requires sustained cold plus new snow. If the 5-day forecast shows multiple warm-precipitation events, adjust expectations accordingly.

The Data Behind This

The model uses multiple station sources, prioritizing actual observations over reanalysis where available:

  • London Ridge Low (2,070m, PCIC station): Primary A-Frame data source. Actual temperature, snowfall, and wind observations with only 170m of elevation adjustment needed. 35 years of data (1990-2025).
  • Open-Meteo ERA5 reanalysis (2,529m grid): Fills gaps where London Ridge Low has no data, and serves as the primary source for Peak elevation.
  • Revelstoke Mountain Resort daily snowfall at 2,225m (13 seasons): Calibrates ERA5 snowfall, which underestimates by 2.5x in complex terrain.
  • Avalanche Canada real danger ratings at 3 elevation bands (1,293 days across 10 seasons): Trains the ML safety model.
  • Environment Canada valley stations: Lodge elevation and valley baseline conditions.
